Student worker unionization in the United States

By Evan Baldonado

June 2023

Student workers at colleges and universities across the United States are unionizing in the pursuit of safe and accessible working conditions, the right to a living wage, and more. If you are a student worker interested in unionizing, this page will tell you what you need to know.

What is unionization?

According to the U.S. Department of Labor, “a labor union is a group of two or more employees who join together to advance common interests such as wages, benefits, schedules and other employment terms and conditions [...] Unions are membership-driven, democratic organizations governed by laws that require financial transparency and integrity, fair elections and other democratic standards, and fair representation of all workers.”

Where is unionization happening?

With whom do student worker unions affiliate?

Generally, individual unions will “affiliate” with a larger, [often] national union. A key principle of unions is that people are stronger together. This same principle can be applied to unions themselves: affiliation allows for resource pooling and unified support. According to the Stanford Graduate Workers Union, “Affiliation with a national union structure allows for workers to pool their resources, which in turn provides them with financial support for workplace actions and campaigns, legal expertise in grievance procedures, and training for rank-and-file union reps, among many other benefits.”

You can see a table of the most common affiliations of student unions below:

Regardless of whether unions are aggregated by system, the most popular six remain the same (although their ordering switches up slightly) and collectively account for >80% of all affiliations.

Also, note that student worker unions can affiliate with nationals that are explicitly academic in scope: take, for example, the American Federation of Teachers (AFT). Other unions, such as United Auto Workers (UAW) and Communications Workers of America Union (CWA) are less evidently academic in nature. Over the years, however, some of these have expanded or changed their scopes to support student workers.

How do student worker unions fight for better pay?

One area in which many student worker unions organize is around pay/compensation for work. This section focuses on UC Berkeley as a case study, although many unions all around the country have achieved similar gains.

The donut chart below shows a breakdown of the costs associated with living as a single adult (with no children) in Alameda County, California (where Berkeley is located). These costs are from the MIT Living Wage Calculator, and they include food, medical, housing, transportation, civic, and miscellaneous expenses, in addition to taxes; together, they sum to $46,356 annually (note: the source data includes a $46,488 required pre-tax income, and it is unclear what accounts for the $132 difference).

To put the pay of a student worker at Berkeley in the context of the cost of living in Alameda County, note that, according to Glassdoor, the estimated average total pay of a Teaching Assistant (TA) at Berkeley is $41,041 annually ($39,059 base pay; $1,982 additional pay; the amount is prorated for students not working the entire year). Fortunately for TAs and other student workers at Berkeley and at other University of California schools, “unions secured a two-and-a-half year contract [in December 2022] that included a 7.5% salary increase for teaching assistants (TAs) and associate instructors” (Trager, 2023). This 7.5% increase applied to an annual pay of $41,041 will result in a new estimated pay of ~$44,119. The three-ringed donut chart below shows this new pay (outer ring) in the context of the current pay (middle ring) and the cost of living (inner ring).

Although the new estimated average pay of TAs at Berkeley still falls short of the costs of living in Alameda County (also note that the cost of living will likely increase due to inflation in the 2.5 years over which the 7.5% increase will occur), it is clear that union-won pay increases will help many student workers at Berkeley and across the UC System.

This is just one example of what a single union can do for student workers in one issue area (pay/compensation). What could your union do for you?

As a student worker, there are many reasons why you may want to consider joining a union. Student worker unions are commonplace around the United States, and there are hundreds of them; you will not be alone in joining a union. Unions provide strength in numbers to support you and your rights as a worker.

You can use the map above to find out more about any union-organizing efforts at your university, and, if there aren't any yet, you can read this guide about how to get started.
